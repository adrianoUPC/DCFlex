## Reviewer #4 Comments

This paper proposes the **LAD-Flex** strategy, which filters loads that can be postponed using task queuing latency, and implements short-term load reduction and peak shifting at the request of aggregators. However, several aspects require further improvement:

1. **Oversimplified GPU power model**  
   The assumption of a linear relationship between GPU power and load is oversimplified, as modern GPUs typically exhibit highly non-linear power curves. Please discuss how this simplification impacts your findings.  
   **Reply to reviewer:**  
   We agree that modern GPUs exhibit nonlinear power-utilization characteristics and that a linear relationship is a simplification. In this study, however, the Alibaba trace does not provide measured power telemetry for each device or a reliable mapping from each task to the utilization trajectory of an individual physical GPU over time. The trace identifies the GPU model/group associated with a task together with its allocated GPU fraction, but not the machine-level operating point needed to calibrate nonlinear power curves specifically for every device. For this reason, we retained a first order linear GPU power model and clarified that the objective of the paper is workload-level flexibility assessment, rather than accurate hardware GPU power characterization. We have revised the manuscript to explain this modeling choice explicitly and to discuss its implications for the interpretation of the results.  
   **Action taken:**  
   We revised the methodology and case study sections to clarify that GPU power is estimated from GPU model/group information and allocated utilization fractions because the dataset does not provide per-device telemetry or reliable task-to-individual-GPU utilization traces. We also added a limitation paragraph stating that the adopted linear model is a first-order approximation of GPU side IT power, that absolute power and energy magnitudes may therefore be misestimated, and that the results are more robust when interpreted in relative terms for flexibility assessment. Finally, we softened the interpretation of the reported power values and added future work indicating that nonlinear node/GPU power models should be calibrated using measured telemetry or machine-level monitoring data when such data become available.  

2. **Discussion of PUE and non-IT power**  
   Since non-IT power accounts for a significant portion of data center energy use, it would be valuable to discuss the implications of the proposed method in terms of **Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE)**.  
   **Reply to reviewer:**  
   We fully agree that non-IT loads and PUE are important dimensions when assessing demand response potential at the whole-facility level. The present study focuses on IT-side flexibility as a necessary first step, since the Alibaba trace provides granular workload-level data but does not include facility telemetry, cooling-system measurements, or dynamic PUE information. This IT-side characterization establishes the workload-level flexibility baseline upon which whole-facility models can be built. We have revised the manuscript to make this scope explicit and to discuss how PUE dynamics and non-IT loads would interact with LAD-Flex activations, providing a clear bridge toward future whole-facility assessments.  
   **Action taken:**  
   We revised the introduction, methodology, results interpretation, and limitations sections to distinguish IT-side flexibility from whole-facility flexibility more explicitly. We now clarify that non-IT systems such as cooling, UPS, and auxiliaries are outside the modeled scope of the present analysis, and that whole-site reduction depends on how these systems respond during flexibility events. We also added a dedicated limitation paragraph discussing potential short-term PUE effects and outlining that future work should combine the workload-level flexibility assessment presented here with facility telemetry or thermal/infrastructure models to characterize whole-site response and dynamic PUE behaviour.  

3. **Workflow dependencies in the Alibaba dataset**  
   In the Alibaba dataset, many tasks within the same group may belong to a single workflow, which is not uncommon. The authors should clarify whether delaying one task could trigger cascading effects on subsequent tasks and, consequently, degrade overall **QoS**.  
   **Reply to reviewer:**  
   The concern about cascading effects within multi-task workflows is legitimate, and we have addressed it by clarifying both the empirical structure of the dataset and the scope of the deferral strategy.  
   First, we quantified the prevalence of multi-task jobs in the cleaned dataset: 97.5% of jobs consist of a single task, with only 2.5% containing multiple tasks. This means that workflow-level dependencies across tasks within the same job are structurally rare in the studied workload. We had stated this qualitatively in Section 3.1 with a forward reference to Section 4, but the actual statistic was missing — we have now inserted it explicitly.  
   Second, we clarified that LAD-Flex defers tasks but does not cancel them: deferred tasks are postponed beyond the flexibility window and still execute. This means that downstream tasks within the same job may experience additional delay, but not permanent blockage or failure. Within the 2.5% of multi-task jobs, cascading delays could in principle propagate, but the scope of this effect is inherently limited by the rarity of such jobs in the dataset.  
   Third, we acknowledge honestly that the Alibaba Cluster Trace does not include explicit dependency metadata, so cascading effects cannot be formally modelled or quantified with the available data. We have added this as an explicit limitation with a clear future-work direction: validating the deferral strategy on dependency-annotated traces, particularly for workloads with tighter inter-task coupling than those observed in this MLaaS environment.  
   **Action taken:**  
   We revised Sections 3.2.2, 4.1, and 6. In Section 4.1, we inserted the quantitative breakdown (97.5% single-task / 2.5% multi-task) to fulfil the forward reference from Section 3.1. In Section 3.2.2, we added a remark that the latency-based deferral classification operates at the individual task level and does not model inter-task dependencies, while noting that multi-task jobs are rare and deferral does not cancel tasks. In Section 6, we added a limitation paragraph acknowledging that the framework treats tasks independently, that cascading delays cannot be formally quantified without dependency metadata, and that future work should validate on dependency-annotated traces.  

4. **Lack of empirical support for \(\alpha\) and \(\tau\)**  
   The parameters \(\alpha\) and \(\tau\) lack empirical grounding in real-world electricity markets. In particular, the assumption \(V_{max} = €2/\text{kWh}\) appears excessively high compared to average bids observed in most European ancillary service markets.  
   **Reply to reviewer:**  
   We agree that the choice of \(V_{\max}\) and the role of \(\alpha\) and \(\tau\) deserved clearer justification, and we have revised the manuscript accordingly. Our response addresses two aspects: the calibration of the valuation bounds and the nature of the shape parameters.  
   Regarding \(V_{\max} = 2\) €/kWh, we acknowledge that this value is high relative to average bids in most European ancillary service markets. However, it is not intended to represent an average balancing price. Rather, it serves as an upper-bound ceiling capturing near-immediate activation under scarcity-driven conditions. Analysis of European balancing activation prices confirms that mFRR activations during system stress can reach price levels of this order of magnitude. \(V_{\min} = 0.05\) €/kWh, in turn, reflects lower-value opportunities consistent with average day-ahead clearing prices in Iberian and Central European markets. Together, these bounds allow \(V(\delta)\) to span the full range from routine energy arbitrage to premium short-notice response. We have expanded the description of these bounds in the manuscript.  
   Regarding \(\alpha\) and \(\tau\), we have clarified throughout the manuscript that these are not empirically estimated coefficients but shape parameters. Because the intermediate decay profile of \(V(\delta)\) is not uniquely determined from available market data, we use \(\alpha\) and \(\tau\) as scenario parameters that are varied systematically to explore how different rates of value decay with notification time affect the optimal flexibility strategy. This is a sensitivity analysis by design, and we have made this framing explicit in the methodology, results, and limitations sections. We have also added a limitation paragraph acknowledging that future work should estimate these parameters empirically, for instance by fitting the decay function to observed flexibility product prices indexed by activation lead time.  
   **Action taken:**  
   We revised Sections 3.3, 4.3, 5.3, and 6 to clarify the empirical grounding of \(V_{\max}\) and \(V_{\min}\), reframe \(\alpha\) and \(\tau\) as scenario parameters for sensitivity analysis, and acknowledge the need for future empirical calibration of the decay profile.

5. **Quantification of the rebound effect**  
   The authors are encouraged to quantify the impact of the **rebound effect** in order to validate the claimed efficiency gains.  
   **Reply to reviewer:**  
   We agree that quantifying the rebound effect is essential and have addressed it by distinguishing two distinct forms of rebound that arise in workload deferral and by introducing a formal metric to quantify the first.  
   The first form is activation-window spillover: energy from deferrable tasks that is consumed outside the contracted flexibility window \([t_1, t_2]\) but belongs to the same activation event. This occurs during the notification period preceding the window (as tasks transition into deferral) and during the buffer period following the window (where the overhang parameter \(\beta\) permits tasks to finish executing). We defined the **Rebound Ratio** (\(RR\)) as the fraction of total activation-related energy that falls outside the flexibility window: \(RR = E_{\text{rebound}} / (E_{\text{def}} + E_{\text{rebound}})\). Results across the three scenarios show that the Rebound Ratio remains between 1.4% and 4.4% (Baseline: 0.044, Intraday: 0.029, mFRR: 0.014), confirming that activation-window spillover is limited and the vast majority of flexibility is delivered within the contracted period.  
   The second form is rescheduling rebound: deferred tasks must eventually execute, consuming the same energy at a later time. Unlike thermal demand-response systems, workload deferral involves no physical penalty — deferred tasks consume identical energy regardless of when they run. The DC operator retains full discretion over rescheduling timing, making this a DC-internal scheduling problem separate from the flexibility assessment. We discuss this component in the limitations section and identify rescheduling strategies as a future work direction.  
   **Action taken:**  
   We added a new analysis within the Scenario Comparison section (Section 5.2.4) that formally defines the Rebound Ratio metric (Eq. 7), presents a new figure showing the Rebound Ratio time series, distribution, and mean values across the three scenarios, and added the Mean Rebound Ratio as a row in the summary table (Table 6). We also revised the limitations section (Section 6) to distinguish activation-window spillover from rescheduling rebound, report the quantitative bounds on spillover, and identify rescheduling strategies under repeated activations as a direction for future work.

   **Reply to reviewer:** We thank the reviewer for this observation.
